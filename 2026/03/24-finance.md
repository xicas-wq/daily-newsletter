## Top Financial Stories

### 1. Trump Says Iran Talks "Productive," Delays Power Plant Strikes
President Trump posted on Truth Social early Monday that he instructed the military to postpone threatened strikes on Iran's power plants after "constructive conversations." He described the discussions as potentially leading to "a complete and total resolution of our hostilities." The news sent oil prices tumbling and triggered Monday's stock rally. But the situation remains fluid --- Iran denies any direct talks, and analysts warn there is "plenty of scope for the war to intensify."

**Why it matters:** The Iran conflict has been the dominant market driver since March began. Oil shock fears have sent tremors through the global economy, with Asian markets (especially South Korea) seeing some of their worst drops in years. Any real de-escalation would be hugely bullish for markets; false hope would set up a painful reversal.

### 2. Fed Holds Rates Steady, Signals One Cut Ahead
The Federal Reserve kept interest rates unchanged at its meeting last week, while signaling it still expects one rate cut this year amid "inflation uncertainty." The decision was widely expected, but the cautious tone reflects the central bank's balancing act between persistent inflation pressures and a job market showing signs of cooling.

**Why it matters:** With mortgage rates at their highest since September and the 10-year yield at 4.33%, borrowing costs remain elevated. The prospect of only one cut (versus the two or three markets had hoped for earlier) means higher-for-longer rates will continue to pressure housing, corporate borrowing, and consumer spending.

### 3. Gilead Buys Ouro Medicines for $2.18 Billion
Pharma giant Gilead Sciences announced a deal to acquire Ouro Medicines for $2.18 billion, gaining access to Ouro's inflammation drug gamgertamig, which is already in the FDA fast-track process. Gilead is also reportedly in talks with Galapagos (GLPG) for a research collaboration using Ouro's assets.

### 4. Tesla Ends 14-Month European Sales Slump
Tesla recorded its first increase in European monthly car sales in over a year, with new-car registrations growing almost 12% in February. This follows last week's data showing Tesla's China retail sales jumped 42% year-over-year. However, competitor BYD saw European registrations nearly triple in the same period.

### 5. Lawmakers Propose Sports Prediction Market Ban
A bipartisan Senate bill would ban prediction markets (like Kalshi and Polymarket) from offering contracts on sports and casino games. The bill targets a loophole where these platforms are regulated federally by the CFTC as "swaps" rather than by state gambling regulators. Traditional sportsbook stocks rallied: Flutter/FanDuel +4.4%, PENN +5.6%, MGM +4.5%, DraftKings +1.2%.

### 6. Broadcom Warns of Growing AI Supply Chain Bottlenecks
Broadcom cautioned that high demand for AI chips is triggering capacity constraints at key manufacturers like Taiwan Semiconductor (TSM). TSMC is expanding capacity through 2027, but in the meantime, bottlenecks in chips, printed circuit boards, and lasers are contributing to longer lead times.

### 7. Estee Lauder Eyes $40B Puig Deal
Estee Lauder is in talks to acquire Spanish beauty company Puig (owner of Charlotte Tilbury). The combined entity could be worth around $40 billion. Estee Lauder shares dropped 8% on the news --- investors may be concerned about the acquisition price and integration challenges.

## Concept of the Day: Relief Rallies

**What is a relief rally?** A relief rally is a sharp, short-term increase in stock prices that happens when negative news turns out to be less bad than expected, or when a feared event appears to be averted. Monday's market surge is a textbook example.

**How it works:** Markets often "price in" worst-case scenarios during periods of uncertainty (like the Iran war). When even a hint of good news arrives --- like Trump saying talks are happening --- traders rush to buy, driving a quick price spike. The key word is *relief*: the rally isn't driven by genuinely improving fundamentals, but by the temporary easing of fear.

**The catch:** Relief rallies are often short-lived. If the underlying problem hasn't actually been resolved (and Iran denying talks suggests it hasn't), prices can quickly reverse. That's why experienced investors are cautious about chasing relief rallies --- they look for *confirmation* that conditions have genuinely improved before adding to positions.

**Connection to today:** Monday's +1.15% S&P rally came on Iran de-escalation hopes. But with futures down this morning after Iran's denial, we may be seeing the classic relief rally pattern play out in real time.
