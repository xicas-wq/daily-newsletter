## Concept of the Day: Stagflation

**What is it?** Stagflation is when an economy experiences **stagnant growth** (or recession) AND **high inflation** at the same time. It's considered one of the worst economic scenarios because the usual policy tools conflict — cutting interest rates to boost growth would worsen inflation, while raising rates to fight inflation would deepen the recession.

**Why does it matter now?** The Iran war is driving oil prices higher (inflationary), while tariffs are raising costs for businesses and consumers (also inflationary). Meanwhile, recession risk is climbing to 30% as economic growth engines weaken. This is the classic setup for stagflation — supply-side shocks (oil, tariffs) pushing prices up while demand weakens.

**Historical parallel:** The most famous stagflation episode was the 1970s, triggered by OPEC oil embargoes. The U.S. experienced double-digit inflation alongside rising unemployment. It took aggressive rate hikes by Fed Chair Paul Volcker (pushing rates to ~20%) to finally break the cycle — at the cost of a deep recession in 1981-82.

**Today's connection:** Project Syndicate's Kalemli-Özcan warns we may be heading down a similar path. The difference is that today's global supply chains are far more interconnected, meaning disruptions cascade faster. The Fed's current "hold and wait" stance suggests they're trying to avoid the 1970s mistake of acting too slowly, but also don't want to crash the economy preemptively.
