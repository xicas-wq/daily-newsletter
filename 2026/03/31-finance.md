### Top Stories

#### 1. Trump Signals Willingness to End Iran War Without Strait of Hormuz
The biggest market-moving headline: The Wall Street Journal reports that **President Trump told aides he is willing to end the US-Iran war** even if the Strait of Hormuz remains closed. While officials cautioned no concrete shift has occurred, the report sent risk assets higher and oil briefly lower. The US could now pursue diplomatic channels to reopen trade through the Strait. Pakistan has offered to host US-Iran peace talks.

However, Trump also posted on Truth Social threatening to "completely obliterate" Iran's electricity plants, oil wells, and Kharg Island if the Strait isn't reopened "shortly" -- highlighting the contradictory signals coming from the White House.

**Why it matters**: Markets have been whipsawed by the Iran conflict for weeks. Any real de-escalation would dramatically ease the oil shock, lower inflation fears, and potentially open the door for Fed rate cuts. Traders stopped pricing in a rate hike after these developments.

#### 2. Powell Says Fed Won't Hike Rates Over Oil Shock
Speaking at Harvard, **Fed Chair Jerome Powell** said rising energy prices from the Iran war probably won't force rate hikes. His reasoning: oil shocks are typically short-lived, and rate changes take too long to kick in -- so they'd only make sense if the public starts expecting permanently higher prices.

This was reassuring: financial markets stopped pricing in a significant chance of a rate increase this year. Just days ago, traders saw a 53% probability of a hike. Powell's dovish tone suggests the Fed will look through the energy shock.

**Why it matters**: The Fed is threading a needle. Inflation is rising (the OECD forecasts 4.2% for 2026), but Powell believes it's temporary. If he's wrong and inflation expectations become "unanchored," the Fed will have to reverse course quickly.

#### 3. Oil Above $100 -- WTI Hits Highest Since July 2022
WTI crude settled at **$102.88/barrel** on Monday, the first time above $100 since the early days of Russia's Ukraine invasion. Brent crude is on track for its biggest monthly gain ever. March has been brutal for energy-dependent sectors.

Gas prices at the pump in the US are surging. The Strait of Hormuz, through which ~20% of world oil transits, remains disrupted. About one-third of globally traded fertilizer also passes through the strait, threatening food prices.

#### 4. Fannie Mae & Freddie Mac Soar After Ackman Call
**Fannie Mae surged 54%** and Freddie Mac rallied after billionaire Bill Ackman called them "stupidly cheap" in an X post. Ackman has been a longtime bull on the government-sponsored enterprises, arguing they should be privatized.

This is a speculative play -- Fannie and Freddie have been in government conservatorship since the 2008 financial crisis. Any privatization would require Congressional action and is politically complicated.

#### 5. McCormick and Unilever Near $60 Billion Food Mega-Deal
McCormick (MKC) and Unilever (UL) are in **advanced talks** to combine food businesses, creating a $60 billion industry giant. Unilever shareholders would own about two-thirds of the new entity. McCormick's stock rose ~4% pre-market, helped by a 16.7% jump in net sales in its latest earnings.

Also in M&A: **Sysco acquired Jetro Restaurant Depot for $29 billion**, the Brooklyn-born restaurant supply chain with 166 stores in 35 states. The combined company will be one of the largest food suppliers in the US.

#### 6. 401(k) Plans May Soon Include Private Credit and PE
The Labor Department proposed a rule to make it easier for employer-sponsored retirement accounts to invest in **alternative assets** like private credit and private equity alongside traditional stocks and bonds. The $12 trillion in Americans' 401(k)s could turbocharge private markets.

Critics warn the timing is terrible: private credit is floundering as AI disrupts the software companies these funds have lent to. Several funds recently limited cashouts. Fees are also much higher than index funds.

### Concept of the Day: Stagflation

**Stagflation** = **stag**nant growth + in**flation** happening at the same time.

Normally, high inflation comes with a strong economy (lots of spending pushes prices up). But sometimes prices rise while the economy slows -- that's stagflation. It's considered the worst-case scenario because the usual tools don't work well:

- **Raise interest rates** to fight inflation? That slows the economy even more.
- **Cut interest rates** to boost growth? That makes inflation worse.

The classic example is the 1970s, when an oil shock (sound familiar?) caused prices to spike while unemployment rose. Today's situation rhymes: oil above $100 from the Iran war is pushing up prices, while consumer sentiment is falling and unemployment may be rising.

Fed Chair Powell is betting the current oil shock is temporary -- meaning he can hold rates steady and avoid the stagflation trap. If he's right, markets will breathe a sigh of relief when oil eventually comes down. If he's wrong, the Fed will face the same impossible dilemma as the 1970s.
