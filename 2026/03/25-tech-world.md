## Hacker News Highlights

### 1. OpenAI Shuts Down Sora (847 points)
OpenAI officially killed its AI video generation app Sora. The internet reacted with a mix of "told you so" and genuine surprise. Kotaku called it the end of OpenAI's "Sora slop farm." Computerworld says this signals an **enterprise-first pivot** — OpenAI is moving away from consumer-facing AI creative tools and doubling down on business applications. The Disney deal rumors also appear dead.

**Why it matters:** This is a significant strategic retreat. Sora was supposed to be OpenAI's answer to video generation, but quality issues and lack of clear monetization killed it. It suggests consumer AI products are harder to sustain than enterprise ones.

### 2. LiteLLM PyPI Packages Compromised (775 points)
**SECURITY ALERT:** LiteLLM versions 1.82.7 and 1.82.8 on PyPI were found to be compromised. This is a supply chain attack targeting one of the most popular AI/LLM proxy libraries. If you're using LiteLLM, check your versions immediately.

This comes alongside a related story: **Andrej Karpathy** (Tesla's former VP of AI) shared an AI coding "horror story" about a Python supply chain attack that could have wiped millions of SSL private keys and database passwords. Elon Musk agreed it's a serious threat.

### 3. Apple Launches "Apple Business" (668 points)
Apple announced **Apple Business** — a new all-in-one platform for businesses of all sizes. This is Apple's most serious enterprise play in years, positioning itself directly against Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace. Details are emerging from Apple's newsroom post.

### 4. Video.js v10: 88% Smaller After 16 Years (481 points)
The creator of Video.js took back the project after 16 years and completely rewrote it, achieving an **88% size reduction**. This is a Show HN success story and a reminder that sometimes the best optimization is a rewrite from scratch.

### 5. Arm Introduces AGI CPU (364 points)

## Thought of the Day

The LiteLLM supply chain attack and Karpathy's warning highlight an underappreciated risk in the AI era: **the code that powers AI systems is itself vulnerable**. As AI tools write more code, and developers trust AI-suggested packages, supply chain attacks become an existential threat. The irony of AI making us more productive while simultaneously creating new attack surfaces is worth reflecting on.
