## Top Stories

### 1. Iran Rejects US Ceasefire Plan — Markets Brace for More Volatility

Iran dismissed the Trump administration's 15-point ceasefire plan (which involved rolling back sanctions and Iran's nuclear program plus reopening the Strait of Hormuz). Iran countered with its own 5-point proposal demanding **reparations and sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz** — conditions the US is unlikely to accept.

**Why it matters for your wallet:** The war continues to drive oil prices higher. The International Energy Agency called this "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market," with a **15 million barrel daily supply deficit**. Pakistan has moved to a 4-day workweek to conserve energy. Countries across Asia and Africa are rationing fuel.

### 3. Meta & YouTube Hit with Landmark Social Media Verdicts

Two major court losses in two days for Meta:

- **California jury** found Meta (Instagram) and YouTube liable for addicting a young woman, awarding **$4.2M against Meta** and **$1.8M against YouTube**. This is a "bellwether case" — it tests the waters for thousands of similar lawsuits in the pipeline.
- **New Mexico jury** ordered Meta to pay **$375 million** for failing to protect minors from sexually explicit content and predators.

Both companies plan to appeal. Notably, **Section 230 did not protect them** — plaintiffs successfully argued the issue was *platform design* (infinite scroll, addictive features), not user-generated content.

## Concept of the Day: Bellwether Case

A **bellwether case** is a lawsuit chosen to be tried first as a test case when there are many similar lawsuits pending. The term comes from the lead sheep in a flock (the one with the bell around its neck) — it shows which way the whole flock is heading.

**How it connects to today's news:** The Meta/YouTube social media addiction verdict was a bellwether case. There are **thousands of similar lawsuits** waiting in the pipeline from parents, school districts, and others. This first verdict gives everyone a signal: juries are willing to hold tech companies liable for *how they design their platforms*, not just what content appears on them.

If you're an investor, this matters because Meta and Google/Alphabet could face **billions in cumulative liability** if these cases keep going against them. It's similar to what happened with Big Tobacco in the 1990s — one verdict opened the floodgates.
