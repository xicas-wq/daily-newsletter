## Concept of the Day: Supply Shock

**What is it?**
A **supply shock** is a sudden, unexpected event that dramatically changes the supply of a product or commodity -- either increasing it (positive shock) or decreasing it (negative shock).

**Why does it matter right now?**
The Iran-Hormuz blockade is a textbook **negative supply shock** for oil. When 20% of the world's oil supply is suddenly threatened, prices spike because demand hasn't changed but supply has dropped. This ripples through the entire economy:

- **Gas prices go up** -- you pay more to fill your tank
- **Shipping costs rise** -- everything transported by truck, plane, or ship costs more
- **Inflation increases** -- the Fed's job gets harder
- **Consumer spending drops** -- people have less money for other things
- **Stock markets fall** -- companies face higher costs and lower consumer demand

**Historical examples:**
- 1973 OPEC oil embargo (negative shock -- triggered recession)
- 2020 COVID shutdowns (negative shock -- supply chains broke)
- 2014 US shale boom (positive shock -- oil prices crashed)

**The key insight:** Supply shocks are particularly dangerous because central banks can't easily fix them. The Fed can lower rates to boost demand, but it can't create more oil or reopen the Strait of Hormuz. This is why the current situation feels so precarious -- the Fed is stuck between fighting inflation and supporting growth.
