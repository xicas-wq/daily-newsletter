## Concept of the Day: Stagflation

**What is it?**
Stagflation is when you get the worst of both worlds: the economy slows down (stagnation) while prices keep rising (inflation). Normally, inflation and economic slowdowns don't happen at the same time — when the economy slows, demand drops, and prices should fall too.

**Why does it happen?**
Usually because of a "supply shock" — something external that makes goods more expensive regardless of demand. The classic example is the 1970s oil crisis, when Arab oil producers cut supply, sending gas prices through the roof while the economy tanked.

**Sound familiar?**
That's exactly what's brewing right now. The Iran war is pushing oil to $92/barrel, driving up gas prices and inflation. But those same high gas prices are squeezing consumers, which could slow the economy. The Fed is stuck:
- **Raise rates** to fight inflation? That could push the economy into recession
- **Cut rates** to help the economy? That could make inflation worse
- **Do nothing?** Both problems could get worse on their own

**How does this connect to today's news?**

This is precisely the dilemma Fed officials are facing. Goolsbee's comment about rate hikes "being on the table" shows they're prioritizing inflation-fighting for now. Goldman's 30% recession probability reflects the risk that this strategy could tip the economy over.

**Historical parallel:** In 1973-74, the Arab oil embargo sent oil prices up 300%. The S&P 500 fell 48% over two years, unemployment doubled, and inflation hit 12%. The current situation is much milder — oil is up ~40%, not 300% — but the dynamics are similar.

**What to watch:** If oil stays above $90 for several months AND economic data weakens (rising unemployment, falling consumer spending), that's the stagflation playbook. If Iran de-escalates and oil drops, this scenario fades quickly.
