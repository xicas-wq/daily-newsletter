## Concept of the Day: Correction vs. Bear Market

You'll hear these terms a lot right now, so let's nail them down:

- **Correction**: A decline of **10% or more** from a recent peak. The Nasdaq just hit this level. Corrections are relatively common — they happen roughly once a year on average.

- **Bear Market**: A decline of **20% or more** from a recent peak. Bear markets are less frequent and usually signal deeper economic problems.

**The key question now is: Will this correction deepen into a bear market?**

Historically, about **one in three corrections** turns into a bear market. The factors that push a correction into bear territory are usually:
1. A recession (not yet confirmed but fears are rising)
2. A major policy error (Fed hiking into a slowdown)
3. A sustained external shock (like a prolonged war disrupting oil supply)

Right now, all three risks are elevated — which is why this correction feels more ominous than usual. But here's the hopeful part: most corrections do *not* become bear markets. The S&P 500 has recovered from 77% of corrections within 4 months.

**Connection to today's news:** The Nasdaq entering correction territory is the market telling us that the combination of the Iran war, sticky inflation, and a hawkish Fed is creating real economic uncertainty. Whether it stays a correction or becomes something worse likely depends on whether the Iran conflict resolves and whether the Fed actually hikes rates.
