### Top Stories

#### 1. Markets See Fed Rate *Hike* for First Time Since 2023
The biggest shift in market expectations: traders now see a **53% probability of an interest rate hike** this year — the first time odds surpassed 50%. The Fed hasn't raised rates since July 2023. While the Fed still officially expects a rate *cut* this year, Chicago Fed President Austan Goolsbee told CNBC he could "see circumstances" for a hike if inflation gets "out of control." The OECD forecasts US consumer prices will climb **4.2% in 2026**, driven by soaring oil. Unemployment is forecast to reach 4.5% in the March jobs report (due Friday).

**Why it matters**: The combination of rising inflation AND rising unemployment is called "stagflation" — the worst-case scenario for the economy. It puts the Fed in an impossible position: raise rates to fight inflation (hurting jobs more) or cut rates to help employment (risking more inflation).

#### 2. Brent Oil on Track for Record Monthly Surge
Brent crude is heading for its biggest monthly gain ever as the Iran war enters its fifth week. The conflict is now expanding:
- **Houthis from Yemen launched missiles at Israel** on Saturday, their first direct participation in the war
- **2,500 US Marines** arrived in the Middle East aboard the USS Tripoli
- **Iran attacked aluminum producers** in UAE (Emirates Global Aluminum) and Bahrain (Alba), causing "significant damage"
- Trump told reporters he could **"take the oil"** and floated seizing Iran's Kharg Island, the country's main oil export terminal

Meanwhile, Iran is profiting: The Economist reports Iran is earning **nearly twice as much** from oil sales daily compared to before the war, using front companies and shadow banks.

#### 3. Global Markets Reel from Middle East Escalation

The sell-off reflects deepening fears that the Strait of Hormuz disruption will persist. About one-third of globally traded fertilizer passes through the strait, with knock-on effects for food prices worldwide.

### Concept of the Day: Correction Territory

**What it means**: A stock market "correction" is when an index falls **10% or more** from its recent peak. Both the Dow and Nasdaq officially entered correction territory last week. The S&P 500 is close at -7% for the month.

**Key distinctions**:
- **Pullback**: A decline of 5-10% — normal and healthy
- **Correction**: A decline of 10-20% — signals real concern, but still recoverable
- **Bear market**: A decline of 20%+ — prolonged downturn, often lasting months

**Historical context**: Corrections happen roughly once every 1-2 years on average. They don't always lead to bear markets — about two-thirds of corrections reverse without hitting bear territory. However, the combination of geopolitical conflict (Iran war), inflation fears, and potential Fed rate hikes makes this correction feel more dangerous than usual.

**Connection to today**: The Dow fell from its January highs of ~50,000 to 45,167 — right at the 10% correction threshold. If the March jobs report on Friday disappoints, the S&P 500 could join the other two indexes in correction territory, making it the first time all three major indexes are simultaneously in correction since 2022.
